# Is the hammer curl vaulted?

Is the hammer curl really vaulted?

The hammer curl and its variations were popularized in the past for being a great brachialis exercise. But due to popular creators stating that it actually doesn't bias the brachialis people stopped doing the exercise "vaulting" it. But is this true and is the hammer curl really a bad exercise?

first we can differentiate what makes a curl variation different than others. this comes down to two factors wrist position and resistance profile. now looking at something like a dumbbell hammer curl we can see that we use a neutral grip opposed to supinated/pronated. and for resistance profile it is ascending up until 90 degrees of the movement. so does this mean it actually is good for the brachialis?

(ascending/neutral) (descending/supinated)

taking the resistance profile into account it aligns with where the brachioradialis has best leverage. and when looking at the wrist position we are disadvantaging the biceps. so overall looking at the two factors the hammer curl is a great brachioradialis exercise. while it's not as perfect as a reverse curl variation it's still a great exercise for the brachioradialis and you should do it if you like it.

(yes your biceps/brachialis still get stimulus)

Elbow flexor moment arm lengths increase with increasing elbow flexion and are maximal in the contracted position. The brachioradialis has very poor leverage when lengthened and the best leverage when shortened.

Biceps brachii moment arm lengths are longer when the wrist is supinated (although only in the middle of the elbow joint angle range of motion). This means that we can therefore target the biceps brachii with supinated grip biceps curls, and the brachioradialis with pronated grip biceps curls.
