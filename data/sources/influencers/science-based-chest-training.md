# Science Based Chest Training

Science Based Chest
Training Explained

the chest is made up of three heads being the clavicular/upper chest, sternal/mid chest and costal/lower chest though the middle/lower are often grouped together as the sternocostal part. the entire chest performs horizontal adduction with the upper chest also performing shoulder flexion. (costal chest also active in shoulder extension/vertical adduction)

horizontal adduction & shoulder flexion

(pectoralis major)

the chest will be best trained with a descending resistance profile with peak challenge towards mid/lengthened range. this is more important in presses with more musculature involved but in flies it won't matter as much since there is less musculature competition for central motor command.

many people ask whether a fly is better than a press. both are effective but a fly will be better as you are eliminating elbow extension but you have to keep in mind if you're not loading above the elbow joint your fly WILL have bicep involvement as well. so if you're loading at the wrist you can align your elbow/wrist to reduce torque at the elbow to reduce triceps in your pressing and biceps in your flies.

so starting off with exercise selection your MAIN exercise that trains all parts of the chest will be your horizontal adduction exercise through a stable machine press or fly.

next you should have another exercise to bias the upper chest. the main purpose of this is to have a tucked arm path to train shoulder flexion. you can also do this through a press or fly.

so overall for the chest you should be performing a flat press/fly to train the entire chest biasing the mid/lower pec and an incline press/fly with tucked arms to train the upper pec.
