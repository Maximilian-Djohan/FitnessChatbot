# what training principles will never change?

What training principles will NEVER change as science based lifting progresses?

some people say that with science based lifting training always changes but what training principles no matter what will never change?

Science-based lifting is an approach to weight training that emphasizes using evidence-based principles to optimize muscle growth and strength gains.

training with intensity with a close proximity to failure will always be important. a high degree of effort and mechanical tension is needed to recruit as many muscle fibers as possible and training close to failure is needed as the last reps in a set will be the most stimulating.

Voluntarily increasing central motor command increases perception of effort, and the number of recruited motor units (and the number of activated muscle fibers).

Strength training volume can probably be best measured by recording the number of reps done with full motor unit recruitment and at slow bar speeds.

also the idea behind exercise selection. this is obvious but no matter what if you want to train a muscle you should be training its muscle actions. and the amount of exercises you do will reflect the muscles YOU care about growing.

the importance of training frequency at 2-3.5x per week won't change as we know subsequent sets will be diminishing, atrophy will occur after ~36-48hrs after training so higher a frequency makes you spend less time in this period and frequency > volume especially if you're training below 2x frequency.

next is the importance of resting. you need CNS fatigue/Cardiovascular sensations to diminish by resting after your set so you won't reach your maximum tolerable perception of effort faster as it would limit central motor command and in turn motor unit recruitment.

The maximum tolerable perception of effort is the point where you stop an exercise set because the feeling of exertion becomes too great, and it is often the limiting factor for performance. Reaching this maximum perception of effort causes task failure.

eliminating stability demand with the use of machines is also important. as a movement is less stable you will have a higher perception of effort from the stabilization demand of your body which in turn will lead to less motor unit recruitment. this means you will be decreasing the amount of muscle fibers that are active/you are training.

Interplay between body stabilisation and quadriceps muscle activation capacity

single joint (eliminating multiple muscles in an exercise) and unilateral exercises will have increased motor unit recruitment due to less overall musculature involved as central motor command will be focused on what's being used as long as you don't suffer from the bilateral force deficit. this is especially the case for bigger muscles that a high voluntary activation deficit (bigger muscles which aren't able to be maximally recruited).

The bilateral force deficit is a phenomenon where the total force produced when using both limbs at the same time is less than the sum of the forces each limb could produce individually.

so as a summary training with intensity/close to failure, frequency, resting, stability, single joint/unilateral are all principles you can apply to your training to benefit. also keep in mind you don't have to utilize these to the highest degree to grow well.
