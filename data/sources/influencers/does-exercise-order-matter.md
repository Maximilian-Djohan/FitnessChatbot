# does exercise order matter?

Does exercise order matter? Does it affect muscle growth?

Many people order exercises based on weight used having more heavier "compound" movements at the start of your workout. Is this actually how you should structure it?

Yes, the order of exercises in a workout does matter, especially when focusing on strength and muscle growth. Generally, you should prioritize compound exercises (those that work multiple muscle groups) and perform them at the beginning of your workout when you are freshest and have the most energy.

there are a few things to look at when ordering exercises being muscle priority, cardiovascular demand, and coordination requirement. muscle priority is the most important for exercise order. you should have your weak points at the start of your session as you will be fresh for them. As you continue to do exercises there will be intrasession fatigue build up which will hinder lifts later in the session.

Another thing to keep in consideration will be the cardiovascular demand of an exercise. an example of this can be seen in leg training. exercises like your leg extension will be less cardiovascular demanding then something like a leg press. having more cardiovascular demanding/fatiguing exercises at the start of your session can have a greater impact on later exercises.

Supraspinal CNS fatigue refers to a decline in the ability of the brain and spinal cord to control and coordinate muscle activity, leading to reduced physical performance and fatigue.

last is the coordination requirement of an exercise in something like a heavier free-weight compound. while cardiovascular demand and muscle priority are important if wanting to progress certain movements that have a higher coordination requirement you can also have those first in session. e.g. bench pressing.

while you could bench later in session having it earlier in session can make the task requirement easier.

so yes exercise order does matter a lot. order exercises based on muscle priority while keeping cardiovascular demand and coordination in mind.
