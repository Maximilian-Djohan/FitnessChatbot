the quadriceps is a group of four muscles that all work to extend the knee. the rectus femoris part of the quad is biarticular where it crosses the hip and performs hip flexion alongside knee extension.

the first factor that influences your training is antagonistic inhibition in the quad. this is where you are lengthening the muscle at one end and shortening it at the other causing it to not get worked "effectively". this is seen in a squat pattern when you are moving at the hip and at the knee causing the rectus femoris to not get trained well.

so what does this mean? you will NEED a leg extension for the rectus femoris of the quads. as the hip angle is fixed you wont actively be shortening/lengthening the rectus femoris at the hip so you will be able to train it when performing knee extension. so do you need a squat pattern?

There are two main studies that people references when comparing squats to leg extensions. throughout this the leg extension is shown to be sufficient for quad growth. but the question is do you NEED a squat pattern? there is no conclusive data that there will be any significant difference including a squat pattern in terms of overall quad growth but there is still more nuance to this.

the second study shows that there is data to show that there could be some regional differences in the distal vastus muscles (regional growth closer to knee) but just based on this it is insignificant. but while we are talking about regional growth another exercise you can implement is pure hip flexion we have activation data that shows the rec fem has more of a "proximal" bias compared to knee extension even when the rec fem was disadvantaged in hip flexion (bad joint angle/isometric placement)

so what does this mean? you just need a leg extension for quad growth. then if you want extra volume you can perform a squat pattern as it will be very effective for the quads and also give more volume to the hip extensors (glutes/adductors) with some possible regional growth to the vastus muscles closer to the knee. and if you want you can even include hip flexion to have some regional rectus femoris growth closer to the hip.

another thing to consider is that in a squat pattern as you are also performing hip extension. the glutes/adductor magnus will be competing with the quads for central motor command. so you can perform something like a peters extension where you are changing hip angle during your leg extension. this will burn off the rectus femoris through antagonistic inhibition to bias the vastus muscles without having to compete with the hip extensors. but this impractical imo as I believe you should just do a normal leg extension.
