# Science Based Bicep Training

Science Based Bicep
Training Explained

your bicep has two heads which both perform elbow flexion. they do this alongside the other elbow flexor muscles such as the brachioradialis and brachialis.

other factors like wrist position, resistance profile and amount of shoulder flexion can help bias muscles in elbow flexion. starting with wrist position having a supinated grip will better train the biceps and as you go more into pronation you will disadvantage the biceps in favor of the brachialis/brachioradialis.

Biceps brachii moment arm lengths are longer when the wrist is supinated (although only in the middle of the elbow joint angle range of motion). This means that we can therefore target the biceps brachii with supinated grip biceps curls, and the brachioradialis with pronated grip biceps curls.

next is resistance profile. your biceps will be best trained with a descending resistance profile where it is hardest at the bottom of the movement. an example of this would be a preacher curl where its hardest at the bottom compared to a free weight curl in which its hardest at the top.

WM. Murray et al. / Journal of Biomechanics 33 (2000) 943-952

next is shoulder flexion angle this just means whether your upper arm is in front of you as in a preacher curl or behind you as in a bayesian curl. there is data that suggests that having your arm in shoulder extension rather than in front of you will give your biceps better leverage.

Comparison Between Shoulder Flexed and Extended Positions in Elbow Flexion Resistance Training on Regional Hypertrophy and Maximum Strength: Preacher versus Bayesian Cable Curls

for a biceps biased curl you should have a supinated grip with a descending resistance profile such as a preacher or recline curl (if you want to be in shoulder extension) during this your brachialis will be trained very effectively.

next for the brachioradialis you can implement an ascending curl where you're using a pronated grip to disadvantage the biceps. this can be done through a reverse curl variation.

so your elbow flexion training should at the very least have a supinated curl variation. and if you want to better train the brachioradialis you can have a pronated curl variation such as a reverse curl.

a side note before I give you exercises recommendations is that the biceps are a small muscle. this means that regardless of what exercise you do as long as you are isolating elbow flexion they will be recruited very well since they won't suffer from the voluntary activation deficit much meaning a "sub optimal" curl will still be effective.

A voluntary activation deficit (VAD) is the central nervous system's inability to fully recruit all motor units in a muscle during a maximal effort.
